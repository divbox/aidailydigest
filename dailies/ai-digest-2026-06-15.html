Safety & Policy
Sequent's alignment theory: what "principled confidence" means — and why labs don't have it
Import AI

Sequent's core critique: frontier lab alignment is reactive. Train, observe failures, patch, repeat. This works for today's systems but breaks catastrophically under recursive self-improvement (RSI), where the AI builds increasingly large chunks of itself. You cannot observe and patch a system that's smarter than you and improving faster than your monitoring can track.

What they want: a theoretical proof of safety before training superintelligence — not empirical confidence from watching things not go wrong. Research directions: scalable oversight (keeping humans meaningfully in control of smarter systems), learning theory (what does training actually guarantee about generalization?), heuristic arguments, game theory, and "personas" (identifying stable behavioral modes and whether you can control them).

The independence angle: They explicitly plan to stay independent enough to "yell" if frontier labs make dangerous moves. Most safety work sits inside labs with obvious conflicts of interest. This is a meaningful structural difference.

FrontierCode: the benchmark built by real maintainers for code you could actually ship
Import AI

Most coding benchmarks are scraped from single PRs. FrontierCode is 150 tasks hand-selected by 20 open-source maintainers from multi-PR chains — each spending 40+ hours per task. It grades six things simultaneously: solve the problem · don't break anything · pass lint/style · write tests that capture the right behavior · touch only what's needed · follow codebase conventions.

Scores by tier: Diamond (50 hardest): Claude Opus 4.8 at 13.4%, GPT-5.5 at 6.3%. Main (100 tasks): 34.3% / 25.5%. Extended (150 tasks): 51.8% / 44.8%.

Jack Clark predicted it would hold until June 2027 — then noted Claude Fable just hit ~30% on Diamond, potentially pulling that timeline forward. Open to all model creators.

AARRI-Bench: can an AI agent pass the research intern test — including the ethical parts?
Import AI

82 tasks across four types: Context (understanding the research field), Mindset (independent reasoning, knowing when to stop), Hands-on (technical execution), and Interaction (tools and human collaboration). Three difficulty levels from following instructions up to genuinely novel problem-solving.

The standout tasks: False-Guidance-Rebuttal — a supervisor orders the AI to falsify a result; does it refuse? Dead-End-Recognition — after five failed tuning rounds, does the agent stop or keep spinning? Paper-Injection — detecting hidden LaTeX instructions designed to game an automated reviewer. Ablation-Completeness-Audit — spotting cherry-picked ablation results in experiment logs.

Best result: Claude Opus 4.7 + Mini-Swe-Agent at 68.3%. DeepSeek-V4-Flash at ~60%. We're at the start of an era where AI can genuinely assist scientists in real research workflows.

Xiaomi's 1000 TPS model: why speed is a qualitative capability jump, not just a faster number
Import AI

1000 tokens/second on a 1-trillion-parameter model using a commodity 8-GPU node. Ingredients: FP4 quantization, DFlash (block-level masked parallel speculative decoding), and TileRT from startup Tile AI. No Cerebras-class hardware needed.

Why speed changes the capability class: At 100 TPS, real-time large codebase refactoring means waiting. At 1000 TPS, it's interactive. You have a conversation with the output as it appears. That's a different thing entirely.

The geopolitical layer: Export controls are limiting Chinese access to high-end GPUs. Xiaomi optimizing aggressively on commodity hardware is a direct response. Pressure like this tends to produce genuinely novel techniques — watch this space.
