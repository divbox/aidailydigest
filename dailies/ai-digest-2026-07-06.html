AIEWF Daily Dispatch: Loops, Software Factories & Forward Deployed Engineers
Latent Space

Day 2 of the AI Engineer World's Fair was dominated by one word: loops. swyx opened with "Loopcraft: The Art of Stacking Loops," tracing AI engineering's arc from chat to tools to goals to, now, automations and cron-style loops. That framing set up the day's main-stage track, Software Factories, anchored by Geoffrey Huntley's "everything is a ralph loop" — the idea of turning a coding agent into a persistent worker by repeatedly restarting it against the same spec.

Multiple speakers put their own spin on the concept. Microsoft's Pablo Castro described a "learning loop" inside Foundry, its AI app/agent factory, where humans and agents co-improve over time. OpenAI's Alexander Embiricos and Romain Huet argued that connecting an agent not just to the work but to why the work matters — and to what happens after, i.e. review and deploy — is what lets it take on more of the pipeline unsupervised. Peter Steinberger, the "ClawFather" behind OpenClaw and now at OpenAI, said his real bottleneck today is deciding what to pay attention to, and that better loops are the fix.

The software factory framing got pushback as much as buy-in. Factory's Tereza Tížková defined it as "the whole lifecycle of developing software with autonomy" — not just coding, but collecting signals, reacting to feedback and logs, prioritizing, and orchestrating. Warp's Zach Lloyd went further, arguing "software engineering will become factory engineering," framing Warp's pivot from CLI tool to factory platform as a bet that hand-written code won't make sense for much longer given how fast these systems can now move.

A parallel thread covered the rise of Forward Deployed Engineers (also called "agent engineers"). Sierra's Natalie Meurer noted that most customer-specific integration work now happens at the orchestration layer, not inside the models themselves. On the open-source side, Z.ai's Zixuan Li previewed the GLM-5.2 model and its ZCode harness (positioned against OpenAI's Codex), while Osmantic's Ahmad Osman argued that once a frontier lab proves a capability is possible, the open-source ecosystem reliably catches up more efficiently.

Vercel's Andrew Qu on why agents are a new kind of software
Latent Space

In this Latent Space conversation, Vercel's Chief of Software Andrew Qu discusses how the company's open-source agent framework, eve, took shape — and why he thinks skills, sandboxes, and agent-readable websites are the pieces that matter now, not bigger models.

eve treats every agent as a directory of files — instructions.md, tools/, skills/ — rather than something instantiated purely in memory, a pattern that Anthropic's Claude Skills and OpenClaw's skills directories have also converged on: the agent is what's on disk. Skills are markdown playbooks loaded only when relevant, and every agent gets its own isolated sandbox for shell commands and file access, keeping agent-generated code — treated as untrusted — out of the application runtime entirely.

We couldn't confirm the direct link to this specific episode at publish time (it wasn't yet indexed), so this entry runs on the Gmail preview text plus Vercel's own eve documentation rather than the full interview. Worth a proper read once the episode page turns up — the framework itself launched publicly at Ship London on June 17, 2026.
