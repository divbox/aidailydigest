OpenAI audits SWE-Bench Pro, retracts its own recommendation to use it
OpenAI ran model-based investigator agents plus five human engineers over SWE-Bench Pro's 731-task public split and found 27.4% of tasks broken by its own pipeline (34.1% per human review), mostly overly strict tests and underspecified prompts. The benchmark had also saturated hard — frontier models climbed from a 23.3% to 80.3% pass rate in eight months, near what OpenAI calls a 70% noise ceiling — so OpenAI is walking back its earlier endorsement of SWE-Bench Pro as the SWE-bench Verified successor. A useful reminder that coding benchmarks decay fast once labs start optimizing against them.

SpaceXAI launches Grok 4.5, first Opus-class model post Cursor acquisition
Latent Space

xAI — now operating under the "SpaceXAI" banner following its acquisition of Cursor — shipped Grok 4.5 on July 8, its first model trained specifically for coding and agentic work rather than general chat. Cursor co-developed it and called it "our most powerful model yet," explicitly distinguishing it from its own smaller Composer line: Grok 4.5 and Composer 2.5 are "two different weight classes," with Composer staying around for lighter-weight future releases. Elon Musk framed the launch not as a benchmark-topping flagship but as "Opus-class... but faster, more token-efficient and lower cost" — a deliberate pitch to the coding-agent workflow market that Anthropic, OpenAI, and Cursor-style tool-using systems have recently dominated.

The numbers back up the efficiency framing more than the raw-capability one. Grok 4.5 is 1.5T parameters, three times the size of Grok 4.3, and prices at $2 per million input tokens / $6 per million output tokens — with a 75% discount on cache hits down to $0.50/1M, though inputs over 200k tokens cost double. Compare that to GPT-5.6 at $5 in/$30 out and Opus 4.8 at $5 in/$25 out. The catch: context window actually shrank to 500k tokens, down from Grok 4.3's 1M, though Musk says a bump back to 1M is coming "by next week."

Independent evaluation from Artificial Analysis put Grok 4.5 at #4 on its Intelligence Index (score 54, +16 points over Grok 4.3), behind only Fable 5, GPT-5.5, and Opus 4.8, and #4 on GDPval-AA v2 (Elo 1543) behind Anthropic's latest Claude releases. It topped the field on τ³-Banking (33% vs. GPT-5.5's 31%) and scored 76 on the Coding Agent Index in Grok Build — on par with GPT-5.5 in Codex, though below Fable 5 in Claude Code. The efficiency story is the more striking part: Grok 4.5 averages ~14k output tokens per Intelligence Index task (60%+ fewer than Opus 4.8) and just 1.9M total tokens per coding-agent task, versus 7.2M for Fable 5 in Claude Code and 6.2M for GPT-5.5 in Codex. Cost per task lands at $0.31 (Intelligence Index), $0.49 (GDPval), and $2.59 (Coding Agent Index) — putting it squarely on the cost/performance Pareto frontier rather than at the capability ceiling.

The release also lands alongside a broader signal that frontier coding benchmarks are running out of room: SWE-Bench Pro, one of the field's most-used coding evals, was independently flagged this same week as having a third of its tasks broken (see the SWE-Bench Pro story in Models & Research). Latent Space's own read is that Grok 4.5 and peers like Cognition's SWE-1.7 are pushing labs to compete on cost-per-task and real-world agent efficiency now that headline pass-rate numbers are saturating.
