Lilian Weng surveys the harness-engineering literature for RSI
Latent Space

Lilian Weng, now a cofounder at AI startup Thinky, published a research recap examining how harness engineering — the orchestration layer around a base model that governs planning, tool use, context management, and evaluation — relates to recursive self-improvement (RSI). The post, published on Lil'Log on July 4, organizes the literature into four buckets: context engineering, workflow design automation, self-improving harnesses, and evolutionary program search, synthesizing roughly 35 papers.

Her central claim is that even as harness improvements get absorbed into core models over time, "the need to specify goals and context will not disappear" — meaning harnesses aren't just a stopgap until models get smarter, but a durable layer of the stack. She traces a throughline from the well-known ACE paper to newer Meta-Harness work, where the harness itself becomes an object of optimization rather than a fixed scaffold around a static model.

The piece landed amid a broader convergence toward this framing: Greg Brockman has been quietly endorsing agent/harness engineering, Sakana connected Weng's argument to The AI Scientist, ShinkaEvolve, and the Darwin Gödel Machine, and LangChain shipped a new Deep Agents course plus an open-source harness project the same week. It also reads as a signal for what Weng's new venture might build next.

Coming from a widely respected interpretability-and-safety researcher rather than an agent-tooling vendor, the piece lends weight to a framing where RSI in practice looks less like models rewriting their own weights and more like increasingly sophisticated orchestration layers compounding gains — with real implications for how labs think about alignment surface area as harnesses take on more of the "thinking."
