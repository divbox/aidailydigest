DeepMind's Pentagon contract backlash resurfaces in a viral thread
Latent Space

Buried in the "Governance and lab politics" section of Latent Space's July 14 AI Twitter recap was a resurfaced controversy: DeepMind senior research scientist Andreas Kirsch (@BlackHC) revisited Google's classified Pentagon AI contract, and the thread drew unusually high engagement, amplified by journalist Carole Cadwalladr. The contract reportedly bars autonomous weapons and domestic mass surveillance "without human oversight," but per employee accounts it also obligates Google to strip technical safeguards that block the Department of War from any "lawful" operational use — and both autonomous targeting and domestic surveillance can qualify as lawful under some circumstances.

This isn't breaking news so much as a flare-up of a slow-burning internal fight. Google quietly removed language pledging to avoid weapons and surveillance technology from its AI principles back in February 2025, with DeepMind CEO Demis Hassabis co-signing a blog post citing "a global competition taking place for AI leadership" as justification. Kirsch's original objection — "I do not understand how this is 'doing the right thing'... this violates 'don't be evil' quite clearly on many levels" — dates to late April, and more than 600 Google employees have signed a letter to CEO Sundar Pichai protesting the deal. DeepMind's UK workers voted to unionize over it in May.

The timing is the interesting part: this thread went viral the same week Hassabis is publicly pushing an AGI governance proposal, endorsed by Sam Altman and Mustafa Suleyman. A DeepMind CEO co-authoring a governance framework for the industry while his own senior researchers publicly excoriate the firm's military contract is the kind of juxtaposition that keeps this story alive months after the original news cycle.

Models & Research
Sakana AI's self-repairing "Smart Cellular Bricks" need no central control
Latent Space

Sakana AI, best known for evolutionary and agent-orchestration software work, published Smart Cellular Bricks in Nature Communications — a move into physical, self-organizing hardware. The system is made of many identical cubes, each running a small onboard neural network and communicating only with its immediate physical neighbors. No cube has a global view or a central controller, yet the collective can infer its own overall shape purely from local signals.

The standout capability is damage detection: individual cells can identify a missing neighbor across any of six spatial directions with 95% accuracy, then coordinate to regrow the target structure — again with no centralized coordination. In simulation, Sakana reports the approach scaling to 18,000+ cubes while retaining this self-repair behavior, suggesting the method isn't just a small-scale proof of concept.

This is a distinct research thread from Sakana's usual output, and it's a genuine cross-source story today — TLDR name-checked "Sakana smart bricks" as one of its three headlines with no further detail, while Latent Space's Twitter recap carried the substantive coverage. The broader relevance is to swarm robotics and self-healing modular structures generally: decentralized shape-inference and damage-repair without a central controller is a hard problem, and a Nature Communications publication with numbers this specific is worth tracking as the field pushes collective intelligence out of pure software.
