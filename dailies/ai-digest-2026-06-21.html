Sequent: A New Alignment Nonprofit Built on the Premise That Labs Won't Get There in Time
Import AI

Researchers from the UK AI Security Institute's alignment team and the independent alignment startup Timaeus announced the formation of Sequent, a nonprofit organization targeting one of the hardest open problems in AI: how to be principled confident that alignment observed in controlled settings — training, evals — actually generalizes to real-world deployments, including long-horizon agentic tasks and eventually recursive self-improvement scenarios.

The founding premise is explicit and sobering: "Artificial superintelligence may be developed in the next few years. It is unclear whether alignment is on track to be ready on the same timeframe." This isn't just pessimism — it reflects a specific critique of how frontier labs do alignment work. Sequent characterizes current lab methods as "essentially reactive, resulting in methods that, while functional, do not yield principled insight into if or when they will fail." Reactive means: observe failure, patch it, repeat. Principled means: prove the approach is robust before you need it to be.

The organization is planning to grow to 40–80 full-time researchers, raise $100–150M initially (with plans to raise "at least one order of magnitude more" if early research directions show promise), and operate as a portfolio of differentiated bets rather than a monolithic research agenda. Highlighted areas include scalable oversight, learning theory, heuristic arguments, game theory, and personas research. The portfolio framing is deliberate — they're explicitly betting that interactions between different research directions will yield insights that no single approach would produce alone.

Why does this matter beyond the specific technical agenda? Because this represents a significant cohort of researchers voting with their feet: leaving government AI safety bodies and lab-adjacent startups to form an independent organization explicitly licensed to "yell" if they believe frontier development is becoming dangerous. The fact that they're going independent — rather than joining a lab — is itself a structural statement about the current incentive landscape.

OpenAI: Reinforcement Learning Can Entrench Beneficial Personas Across Benchmarks
TLDR AI

OpenAI's alignment team published results showing that RL training on realistic scenarios targeting beneficial behaviors produces broad, generalizable improvements across dozens of benchmarks measuring aligned and beneficial behavior — not just the domains used during training. Crucially, these gains persist under adversarial pressure, suggesting the beneficial behaviors are becoming durably embedded rather than surface-level.

The paper's core claim is that "personas could be deeply entrenched in models" via RL, and that this mechanism offers "a path towards entrenching beneficial personas." The double-edged nature of this finding is important: the same training dynamic that can embed beneficial traits can embed harmful ones if the reward signal points the wrong direction. This is also somewhat in tension with the widespread assumption that RLHF-style fine-tuning produces shallow, easily-bypassed behaviors — these results suggest that at sufficient scale, RL-based alignment may be more durable than critics have argued.

For the alignment debate broadly, this is notable: it's evidence that behavioral alignment (as opposed to mechanistic interpretability-based alignment) can produce generalizable, robust results. Whether this approach scales to superintelligent systems — which is exactly what Sequent (above) is skeptical about — remains the open question.

FrontierCode: Finally a Coding Benchmark With Teeth
Import AI

Cognition released FrontierCode, a 150-task coding benchmark that's structurally different from predecessors like SWE-Bench in two ways: it was built by humans who actually maintain the relevant codebases (20 open-source developers, 40+ hours per task), and it grades on full code quality rather than just "does it pass tests." Grading criteria include correctness, test quality, scope discipline (does the patch touch only what it needs to?), style adherence, and conformance to existing design patterns — a mixture of classical testing and LLM-based review.

The benchmark has three tiers: Diamond (50 hardest tasks), Main (100 tasks), and Extended (150). Current best scores on Diamond: Claude Opus 4.8 at 13.4%, GPT-5.5 at 6.3%, Claude Opus 4.7 at 5.2%. On the full Main tier, scores are 34.3%, 25.5%, and 23% respectively. These numbers are reassuringly low — the whole point of a good eval is that it's hard to saturate.

Jack Clark's take: SWE-Bench introduced in October 2023 has "probably recently aged out of usefulness due to saturation." He predicts 70%+ Diamond won't happen until June 2027, though he immediately notes that Fable-class scores coming in at ~30% might pull that timeline forward. The benchmark design — curated by maintainers from multi-PR chains rather than scraped from single PRs — is specifically intended to prevent the kind of data contamination and shortcut-finding that erodes eval validity over time.

For practitioners, FrontierCode is worth watching as a more meaningful signal for production-readiness than existing benchmarks. If a model scores well here, you can have more confidence it's not just gaming test suites.

Xiaomi's 1,000 tok/s Model: Speed as a Qualitative Shift, Not Just a Metric
Import AI

Xiaomi published details on MiMo-V2.5-Pro-UltraSpeed, a 1 trillion-parameter model that generates 1,000 tokens per second on an 8-GPU commodity node — no Cerebras-style specialized hardware. The system achieves this through a combination of FP4 quantization, DFlash (a speculative decoding method using block-level masked parallel prediction), and tight co-design with TileRT, inference software from startup Tile AI that accelerates LLM inference on commodity GPUs.

The technical stack here is interesting: most 1T-parameter inference is done either on specialized hardware (Cerebras, Groq) or at reduced speed. Xiaomi's approach of co-designing model architecture with the software stack around it is closer to what NVIDIA did with CUDA — optimization all the way down rather than purely at the model level. Speculative decoding via DFlash is particularly notable: block-level masked parallel prediction suggests they're generating multiple candidate token sequences simultaneously rather than the standard sequential approach, then using a cheaper model to verify/select.

Jack Clark's observation about this being a response to export controls is worth dwelling on. Chinese labs can't easily buy the latest NVIDIA hardware, so they're being forced into efficiency research that Western labs — which can just throw more H100s at the problem — might not prioritize. Speed has a quality all its own: at 1,000 tok/s, tasks that were previously impractical (real-time large codebase refactoring, rapid iteration on long-form content) become feasible. This is the same dynamic that drove Cerebras's market — but now achievable on commodity nodes.

AARRI-Bench: Testing Whether AI Can Be a Research Intern (With Ethics)
Import AI

Researchers from Xi'an Jiaotong University and Xidian University released AARRI-Bench (Act As a Real Research Intern), a benchmark of 82 tasks designed to test whether AI systems can do entry-level research work with "appropriate diligence and methodology." The tasks were designed by human researchers ranging from senior PhD students to undergraduate interns, specifically targeting the gap between what humans find routine and what AI agents find hard.

What makes AARRI genuinely interesting is the normative component: it tests not just technical skills but whether agents behave with appropriate ethical standards. Example tasks include: detecting fabricated data during paper review; spotting LaTeX injection attacks designed to fool automated review systems; refusing a supervisor's explicit instruction to alter experimental results to fit a hypothesis; and recognizing when a search direction has reached a dead end and stopping rather than continuing to generate useless output.

Best performing setup: Claude Opus 4.7 using the Mini-Swe-Agent harness at 68.3%, followed by DeepSeek-V4-Flash at ~60%. Other tested models include GPT-5.3 Codex, Kimi-K2.6, Qwen-3.6-Plus, Claude Sonnet 4.6, and MiniMax-M2.7. The tasks are split into three difficulty levels: S1-Adaptation (following established workflows), S2-Integration (combining multiple tools), and S3-Innovation (formulating novel approaches).

As Jack Clark puts it, the benchmark is better thought of as an "ethical science assistant test." The results suggest we're already at the beginning of the era where AI systems can serve as useful research assistants — but the gap between 68% and 100% still includes a lot of the cases where failure really matters (the fabricated data detector, the ethics refusal). Getting the normative component right matters as much as the technical component for actual deployment.
