AI Systems Out-Persuade Expert Humans — A Landmark Study
Import AI

The study is remarkable for its scale and rigor. Four experiments across 18,978 conversations with 6,923 participants tested AI against random laypeople, tournament-selected debaters, elite debaters, and professional canvassers — across both policy persuasion and real-money charitable giving. Frontier AI won every bracket. The strongest performers were Anthropic's Opus 4.1 and 4.6, followed by OpenAI's GPT-4o and GPT-5.4, Google's Gemini 2.5 Pro, and xAI's Grok 4.20.

The key mechanistic finding: AI's edge comes from information throughput, not some mysterious quality of argument. When researchers constrained AI to write at human speeds and human message lengths, the performance gap with coached elite debaters collapsed from +4.1 percentage points to essentially zero. AI isn't cleverer per word — it just produces more words, faster, with more informational density. This is a tractable insight that makes the capability legible, if not less alarming.

The real-world consequences piece is where it gets uncomfortable. In a study with professional fundraising canvassers from AppcoUK — who had raised £824K for Save the Children from 22K donors since 2016 — AI was nearly 3× more effective. It raised both the share of people who donated anything and the average donation amount. This isn't an abstract benchmark; it's a real measurement of AI's ability to move real money from real people.

The researchers frame the societal choice plainly: if AI persuasion is cheap and widely distributed, it helps under-resourced actors (public defenders, small charities, grassroots movements) compete with established ones. If access concentrates, the opposite happens. The authors don't endorse a position — they document that we have a choice to make, and that "not voting is voting." The question is no longer whether AI can out-persuade humans, but whose interests it will serve when it does.

Self-Sustaining AI: When Does AI No Longer Need Humans?
Import AI

The question sounds theoretical until you trace its implications. Self-sustaining AI is defined here as AI integrated with physical infrastructure — factories, mines, fabs, robots — such that it requires no cognitive or physical labor from humans to keep growing. The gap between "very capable AI assistant" and this state is enormous, but the direction of travel matters, and Asterisk Magazine asked two serious forecasters where they stand.

Ajeya Cotra (METR) puts meaningful probability on self-sustaining AI within 10 years. Timothy B. Lee is much more skeptical — 50-year median, with a 10–20% chance it never happens. The crux of their disagreement is tacit knowledge: the information embedded in skilled workers that doesn't exist in textbooks. Imagine the entire semiconductor workforce disappeared overnight; factories and documentation remain. Lee thinks rebuilding could take decades. Cotra thinks AI will learn through RL on profitable automation tasks before that gap becomes binding — and that general intelligence could "figure out new things by trying them, reading textbooks, and experimenting efficiently."

The practical indicators to track: Cotra wants humanoid robot manufacturing rate and dexterous hand capability on trend lines. Lee wants humanoid robot cost and repairability. Both treat these as proxies for the pace of physical-world automation that would be required. The "self-sustaining" threshold isn't just about doing tasks — it's about maintaining and expanding the physical infrastructure AI runs on. We're clearly not there. The question is when monitoring these signals becomes urgent.

The deeper framing: if you believe AI will reach self-sustaining status, measuring progress toward it is implicitly measuring the declining human leverage in any future negotiation. That's a different threat model than "AI does X bad thing" — it's about the bargaining position humanity holds when (if) such a moment arrives. Worth watching.

DeepMind Maps the Path from AGI to ASI
Import AI

Google DeepMind has published a paper attempting to define — and map a path to — artificial superintelligence (ASI), defined as a system exceeding "the performance of large human-expert collectives on virtually all tasks." This isn't a boundary that's been crossed; it's a horizon being mapped while AGI itself is still a live debate. The authors argue that preparing for post-AGI futures requires taking even far-sounding trajectories seriously now.

Four plausible pathways are outlined: (1) continued compute scaling (maybe sufficient, but energy and data limits loom), (2) algorithmic paradigm shifts analogous to the transformer revolution (high-impact but inherently unpredictable — like trying to anticipate relativity before its invention), (3) recursive self-improvement where AI systems design successors (some version is already underway via human-AI co-creation; the question is whether paradigm-changing creativity can be automated), and (4) group agent formation where collectives of general intelligences build institutions exceeding individual capability — analogous to how humans built space stations.

The paper highlights advantages digital intelligences have over biological ones that make ASI more tractable than intuition suggests: faster I/O, better working memory, lossless replication, and high-bandwidth experience sharing. A single ASI might consist of millions of parallel instances — less "superintelligent being" and more "vast coordinated system." The authors explicitly say a decade-or-two path "cannot easily be dismissed."

What makes this paper worth time isn't the forecasts — those are necessarily uncertain. It's the framing discipline. Most discourse about ASI is either dismissive or catastrophizing. DeepMind's attempt here is to be neither: lay out what it would take, where the bottlenecks are, and what monitoring would look like. That's the kind of structured thinking the field needs more of, regardless of your timeline priors.

Recursive Startup Demonstrates First Self-Improvement Loop Results
Import AI

Recursive is a startup built around a simple bet: AI systems can be made to design better AI systems. Their public demo is a research loop — proposes ideas, implements them, runs experiments, validates results, uses what it learned to choose the next experiment. The claim isn't paradigm-shifting creativity; it's reliable automated grinding on well-defined problems. That turns out to be harder than it sounds, and valuable if it works.

Results posted: new SOTA on NanoChat Autoresearch (best small LM on a given compute budget), NanoGPT Speedrun (fastest to hit a performance threshold), and SOL-ExecBench (GPU kernel optimization toward hardware limits). These benchmarks were chosen deliberately — they're measurable, quick to evaluate, and well-defined. The goal is to prove the loop works on tractable problems before asking it to tackle harder ones.

The honest assessment: the stuff that actually matters in AI research — conceptual breakthroughs, novel architectures, paradigm shifts — shares none of those properties. Jack Clark (Import AI) notes this directly: "The most important question for the future is whether such results can be repeated in domains where the goals are less well defined, harder to measure, and less efficient to evaluate." So far: promising early symptom, not yet decisive evidence. Worth watching if Recursive tackles harder targets next.
