Fable writes the fastest submitted GPU megakernel on KernelBench-Mega
Import AI

Fable has written what a KernelBench-Mega maintainer calls the first genuine — and fastest — megakernel ever submitted to the benchmark. Writing raw CUDA for an RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell, it achieved an 18.71x speedup over an optimized PyTorch baseline. For calibration: Claude Opus 4.8 hit 14.4x writing Triton, GLM-5.2 got 11.14x (also Triton), and GPT-5.5 managed 4.34x.

What makes the solution notable isn't just the speedup — it's the shape of it. Per analysis from one of the benchmark's maintainers, torch.profiler shows exactly one cooperative kernel launch per decoded token. Every other high-scoring entry decomposed the problem into 4 to 14 separate kernel launches per token. That's a structurally different (and harder) solution, not just a faster one.

Kernel design sits close to the metal of AI R&D — it's one of the fundamental tasks involved in building and optimizing the systems that train and run models. Jack Clark's framing: the better models get at tasks like this, the better they get at the kinds of tasks needed for AI development generally, which is the raw material of a recursive self-improvement loop. Benchmarks like KernelBench-Mega are worth watching less for the leaderboard and more as an early gauge of how close models are getting to improving themselves.

OSWorld 2.0 benchmarks multi-hour computer-use agents
Import AI

A large multi-institution team (HKU, UCSD, Columbia, UCSB, Mila, Snorkel AI, Wisconsin, Alibaba Qwen, Ohio State, Simular, NeoCognition) has released OSWorld 2.0, a benchmark for multi-step, multi-program computer-use tasks. The jump in difficulty from version 1.0 is stark: median task time for a human went from 2 minutes to 1.6 hours — a 48x increase — and 69.6% of tasks are estimated to take a skilled human over an hour.

The software surface expanded to match. Where OSWorld 1.0 shipped with LibreOffice, GIMP, VLC, Thunderbird, VS Code, and Chrome, 2.0 adds Slack, LinkedIn, GitLab, Overleaf, AWS, REAPER, MuseScore, WPS, LabPlot, Zotero, and simulated professional-service portals for things like insurance claims and visa applications. Task categories span document prep, database work, finance/ops analysis, admin support, sales and customer support, and graphic presentation.

Current models are still far from reliable here: the best configuration tested — Claude Opus 4.8 with maximum thinking and batched tool calls — reaches only 20.6% binary accuracy and 54.8% partial-score accuracy. Performance drops sharply as tasks lengthen, especially when agents need to recover hidden state, track many items, or adapt to changing requirements.

Worth noting: OSWorld 1.0 saw scores climb from ~30% (July 2025) to ~75% (MiniMax M3, June 2026) over roughly a year. If 2.0 follows a similar ramp, today's 20% floor won't hold for long.

Remote Labor Index success rate jumps from 2.5% to 16.1% in nine months
Import AI

Researchers at the Center for AI Safety and Scale Labs report a sharp rise in AI systems' ability to complete full, economically valuable freelance-style projects end-to-end, per their Remote Labor Index (RLI). Success rates went from 2.5% at the benchmark's October 2025 launch to 16.1% in a July 2026 update — "more than quadrupled in under eight months," per the authors.

The July update tested three frontier models: GPT-5.5 (6.3%), Opus 4.8 (8.3%), and Fable 5 (16.1%), the current leader. RLI tasks are deliberately varied and real-world: examples include re-modeling a client's engagement ring in a different cut with photorealistic renders, producing a full animated advertisement synced to a supplied voiceover, and turning a scanned cadastral plan into a dimensioned floor plan with furniture layouts and renders — full deliverables, not toy problems.

Jack Clark's read is blunt: it's getting harder to reconcile this trajectory with the economy staying the same. His bet is that AI capability growth and models' increasing fluency with the same software tools humans use are outpacing how fast humans can create new comparative advantage for themselves — meaning person-light, AI-heavy organizations are more likely to expand and out-compete unaugmented humans than the reverse.

JD's Oxygen AI Item Center manages inventory across billions of SKUs
Import AI

JD — the Chinese e-commerce platform with 700 million users, millions of merchants, and a catalog of tens of billions of SKUs — has published details on Oxygen AI Item Center (Oxygen AIIC), the system that keeps its inventory catalog coherent. It now covers tens of thousands of JD categories and processes hundreds of millions of item updates per day, running on Huawei Ascend NPUs rather than Nvidia hardware.

The system has four core pieces: a human-AI collaborative ontology-engineering process where experts distill domain knowledge and algorithms scale it up; a "semantic search then discrimination" pipeline that externalizes the ontology into a separate knowledge base so it can update without model retraining; self-evolving item-understanding LLMs/VLMs that use lightweight "expert modules" to add capability incrementally without catastrophic forgetting; and a "unified item tunnel" interface that serves daily, minute-, and second-level pipelines while keeping data consistent.

The choice of Huawei Ascend NPUs is itself a signal — part of China's broader push toward compute self-sufficiency, run at a scale where the compute platform is described as one of the system's two primary technical challenges (the other being efficient resource use at scale).

The bigger picture: Oxygen AIIC is a working example of intelligence woven directly into back-office functions like inventory management, letting a business operate at far larger scale than would be possible with human-run processes — and to keep learning and updating largely without direct human oversight.
