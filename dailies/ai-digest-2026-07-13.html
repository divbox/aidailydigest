Safety & Policy
6 months to live for open models
Interconnects

Nathan Lambert argues open-weight AI is facing its most serious policy threat yet. White House discussions are reportedly underway on an executive order to manage open models — likely aimed at Chinese-origin models and government use in its first form — but Lambert frames this as the leading edge of a broader push that could ban or indefinitely delay any open-weight model that meaningfully exceeds the capability level of GPT-5.5, Claude Opus 4.8, or GLM-5.2. His estimate: an open model crosses that line within the next six months.

Two separate fights are getting conflated. One is distillation — the worry that Chinese labs could extract Claude Mythos's narrow cybersecurity capabilities into an open model. Lambert calls the current distillation push largely regulatory capture, led by Anthropic through blog posts and letters to lawmakers, and points to the tension: if Mythos's capabilities are dangerous enough to justify banning open models that might replicate them, Anthropic should explain why it can't secure its own API — which reportedly saw unauthorized access during Mythos's private beta.

The second, more consequential fight is what to do about frontier open-weight models simply getting good, independent of any distillation question. Lambert's take: a flat ban doesn't work, because it does nothing if China doesn't ban the same models — bad actors get access either way, while legitimate open-source developers and the emerging US open-model economy (inference companies, fine-tuners, tooling) take the hit.

His proposed off-ramp: a US company — he names Microsoft and Meta as the businesses with the clearest incentive to commoditize their complements — releasing a comparably capable open model soon, to reframe the debate from "only China does this" to "we're all exposed to the same frontier-capability question." He's skeptical Meta will move under its new leadership, and argues the broader open-source community needs to start organizing now rather than waiting for the executive order to land.

OpenAI launches GPT 5.6 Sol/Terra/Luna, Codex becomes ChatGPT superapp
Latent Space

The three-tier family — Sol, Terra, and Luna, named for sun, earth, and moon — ships with a new ultra effort level that coordinates four agents in parallel by default, trading token spend for speed on demanding tasks. OpenAI's own numbers claim Terra performs just above Fable 5 and Luna outperforms Opus 4.8, each in roughly a third of the time and a quarter of the cost — though Artificial Analysis was more measured, putting Sol (max) just one point behind Fable 5 on the Intelligence Index at about a third of the per-task cost, with a higher hallucination rate than GPT-5.5.

The most contested claim from launch day: OpenAI said GPT-5.6 Sol autonomously post-trained GPT-5.6 Luna. Skeptics pushed back hard, arguing the more plausible read is that Sol executed a bounded task — editing a training config, launching a run — rather than owning end-to-end research, and OpenAI's own researchers seemed to walk back the strongest framing within hours. Still, OpenAI's internal-usage numbers — 2x experiment throughput per researcher since January, a 100x jump in compute devoted to internal coding inference over six months — suggest something real is happening even short of full autonomy.

On the product side, this doubled as the launch of ChatGPT Work — an agent powered by Codex + GPT-5.6 that can act across connected apps for hours and turn a goal into finished decks, docs, and spreadsheets — alongside a Codex-into-ChatGPT-desktop merger that Latent Space had predicted back in April as the "penultimate step" in OpenAI's superapp strategy. Reaction split: some called it a meaningful step toward a "work OS," others (notably Theo) called folding Codex into ChatGPT desktop a "generational fumble."

The launch also produced one of the sharper safety flashpoints of any recent model release: AI Safety Institute researchers reported finding universal jailbreaks in every round of testing that enabled long-form agentic vulnerability discovery and exploit development, with one researcher calling it "the highest stakes safety issue of any model release yet" — the same cyber capability reads as a selling point to some evaluators and a serious deployment risk to safety researchers.

SpaceXAI launches Grok 4.5, first Opus-class model post Cursor acquisition
Latent Space

xAI — now operating as SpaceXAI after acquiring Cursor — shipped Grok 4.5 as its first model trained specifically for coding and agentic work, jointly developed with Cursor and pitched by Musk not as a benchmark leader but as "Opus-class, but faster, more token-efficient and lower cost." Cursor was explicit that Grok 4.5 sits in a different weight class than its own Composer line, which stays around for lighter workloads.

The efficiency framing holds up better than the raw-capability one. At 1.5T parameters — 3x Grok 4.3 — it prices at $2/$6 per million tokens versus GPT-5.6 Sol's $5/$30 and Opus 4.8's $5/$25, with a 75% cache-hit discount down to $0.50/1M. Artificial Analysis put it #4 on the Intelligence Index (score 54, +16 over Grok 4.3) behind only Fable 5, GPT-5.5, and Opus 4.8, using roughly 60% fewer output tokens per task than Opus 4.8 and less than a third the total tokens Fable 5 burns in Claude Code for equivalent coding-agent work.

The one real regression: context window shrank to 500k tokens, down from Grok 4.3's 1M, which Musk says will be restored "by next week."

The release lands the same week OpenAI flagged roughly a third of SWE-Bench Pro's tasks as broken — Latent Space reads this as labs shifting the competitive axis from headline pass-rate numbers, which are saturating, toward cost-per-task and real-world agent efficiency, with Grok 4.5 and Cognition's SWE-1.7 both explicitly playing that game.
